# GE Aerospace 10-K FY2025 (excerpt — investment summary)

## ธุรกิจหลัก
GE Aerospace (spun off from General Electric in 2024) is a leading aerospace propulsion and systems company. The company designs, manufactures, and services jet engines, avionics, and critical aircraft systems for commercial and defense customers. GE Aerospace serves customers in approximately 120 countries and operates manufacturing and service facilities at 70 locations in the US and 62 locations in 23 other countries. The business model combines high-margin aftermarket services (long-term service agreements, MRO, spare parts) with equipment sales, creating recurring revenue streams.

## Product lines
- **Commercial Engines**: LEAP engines for commercial aircraft, other derivative commercial engines
- **Defense Engines**: F110 engines, F404 engines, next-generation engine programs
- **Aerospace Systems**: Avionics, power systems, embedded software solutions
- **Aftermarket Services**: Maintenance, repair and overhaul (MRO), spare parts, long-term service agreements spanning 10–25 years

## Segments
1. **Commercial Engines & Services (CES)** – 77% of total revenue
   - Supplies commercial jet engines and provides aftermarket services
   - Key customers: Boeing, Airbus, regional aircraft manufacturers, airlines, MRO centers
   
2. **Defense & Propulsion Technologies (DPT)** – 23% of total revenue
   - Defense & Systems (D&S): Military jet engines, avionics, power systems
   - Propulsion & Additive Technologies (P&AT): Advanced materials, additively manufactured components

## Distribution
- **Direct sales**: Long-term contracts with airframers (Boeing, Airbus) and airline customers
- **Aftermarket**: Internal and external MRO networks, spare parts distribution to airlines and third-party MRO providers
- **Government contracts**: Defense & Systems revenue primarily flows through U.S. DoD budget and international government budgets

## การแข่งขัน
GE Aerospace faces competition from Rolls-Royce (commercial engines), Pratt & Whitney (commercial engines), and other defense prime contractors. Market position:
- Leading supplier of commercial jet engines (LEAP dominates modern narrow-body market)
- Strong aftermarket position due to long-term service agreements and growing MRO network
- Significant government relationships and funding for defense products
- High barriers to entry: extreme capital intensity, certification requirements, decades of domain expertise

## Supply chain — ความเสี่ยง
- **Supply chain constraints**: Material sourcing (titanium, nickel-based alloys) and component supply delays cited as a constraint on production growth in 2025
- **Inflation and labor costs**: Higher labor and material costs partially offset by pricing actions
- **Tariffs**: Unfavorable impact on long-term service agreement profitability from estimated tariff effects
- **Geopolitical**: U.S.-China tensions, trade sanctions, defense program shifts affect demand
- **Supplier concentration**: Reliance on a network of partners for component design and manufacturing

## Seasonality
Minimal strong seasonality noted in the 10-K. Commercial engine deliveries and aftermarket services follow global air travel cycles, which peak in summer and holiday periods but are relatively smooth throughout the year. Government defense spending is less seasonal. Orders and backlog (RPO of $190.6B as of Dec 31, 2025) provide forward visibility.

## Human capital
At December 31, 2025, GE Aerospace employed approximately **57,000 people** (~30,000 in the United States). The company invested $1 billion in U.S. manufacturing and hired 5,000 U.S. workers in 2025, reflecting strategic capacity expansion. The workforce spans engineering, manufacturing, quality, supply chain, and aftermarket service roles.

## IP
- **Patents & Trademarks**: Extensive proprietary technology in advanced jet engine design (LEAP, F110, F404), high-temperature materials, and avionics systems
- **Certification & Regulatory**: FAA and international certifications are key regulatory IP
- **Development Agreements**: Cost-share arrangements with customers (Boeing, Airbus) and partners fund R&D

## FY2025 Financial Summary

### Revenue drivers
- **Commercial Engines & Services (CES)**: $33,314M (+24% YoY from $26,881M)
  - Equipment: $8,304M (+17% from $7,106M) — increased engine deliveries and pricing
  - Services: $25,010M (+27% from $19,775M) — higher spare parts, internal shop visits, workscopes
  - Growth drivers: LEAP engine demand, commercial air travel recovery (departures +3%), aftermarket expansion
  
- **Defense & Propulsion Technologies (DPT)**: $10,554M (+11% from $9,478M)
  - D&S: $6,574M (+8% from $6,109M) — increased engine deliveries, aircraft systems growth
  - P&AT: $3,980M (+18% from $3,370M) — volume and price growth
  - Growth drivers: U.S. DoD modernization, foreign military sales (new IDIQ contract up to $5B), F110/F404 demand
  
- **Corporate & Other**: $1,987M (-15% from $2,343M) — insurance revenue and eliminations

### Gross margin
- **Equipment segment**: Slight loss: $(223)M on $12,159M sales = **-1.8% margin** (vs. +2.1% estimated for CES at 26.6% operating)
- **Services segment**: $13,577M gross profit on $30,163M = **45.0% gross margin** (strong aftermarket economics)
- **Operating profit (total)**: $10,000M pre-tax on $45,855M revenue = **21.8% margin** (improved 210 basis points YoY)

**Segment Operating Margins:**
- CES operating margin: 26.6% ($8,861M profit on $33,314M revenue) — up 40 bps from 26.2% in 2024
- DPT operating margin: 12.3% ($1,296M profit on $10,554M revenue) — up 110 bps from 11.2% in 2024

Margin expansion driven by volume growth, pricing, and mix, partially offset by inflation and tariff headwinds on long-term service agreements.

### Operating expenses
- **SG&A**: $4,088M (8.9% of revenue) vs. $4,437M in 2024 (-8%)
- **R&D**: $1,580M (3.4% of revenue) vs. $1,286M in 2024 (+23%) — increased investments in next-generation products
- **Interest & other financial charges**: $843M vs. $986M in 2024 (-14%) — lower interest on tax deficiencies

### Liquidity & capital return
- **Cash & equivalents**: $12,392M (down from $13,619M; includes $1.1B in restricted cash for litigation)
- **Investment securities (total)**: $38,788M non-current + $0M current = **$38,788M** (down slightly from $37,741M + $982M = $38,723M)
- **Total cash + securities**: $51,180M
- **Total debt**: $20,494M ($1,686M short-term + $18,808M long-term), up from $19,273M
- **Net cash**: $30,686M (healthy position)
- **Capital returned to shareholders**: $9,003M total
  - Buybacks: $7,551M (up 30% from $5,827M)
  - Dividends: $1,452M (up 44% from $1,008M)
- **Free cash flow (non-GAAP)**: $7,694M in 2025 (up from $6,203M in 2024)
- **Operating cash flow**: $8,537M continuing operations; $8,543M reported for continuing ops

### Tax
- **Effective income tax rate (continuing ops)**: 14.1% in 2025 vs. 12.6% in 2024
- **Provision for income taxes**: $1,405M on $10,000M pre-tax income
- Higher 2025 rate reflects tax provisions on pre-tax income; both years represent favorable rates due to tax planning and benefits

## Key Risk Factors

### Macro & geopolitical
- **Inflation & supply chain**: Elevated costs for materials, labor, and components; supply constraints on critical items (titanium, nickel alloys) still limiting production growth
- **Tariffs & trade tensions**: Unfavorable impact on long-term service agreement profitability from estimated tariff effects mentioned in 2025 results; U.S.-China tensions affecting supply chain
- **Recession & demand cycles**: Commercial air travel subject to macroeconomic cycles; defense spending may shift with government priorities
- **Geopolitical conflicts**: Potential impact from major wars, sanctions, or trade disruptions affecting suppliers and customers

### Regulatory & legal
- **Certification & compliance**: FAA, EASA, and international certifications required; changes in regulatory requirements could delay programs
- **Government contracts**: Defense business (~23% of revenue) dependent on U.S. DoD and international government budgets; contract losses or funding cuts pose risk
- **Environmental regulations**: Potential pressure for lower-emissions engines and sustainability requirements
- **Litigation**: Run-off insurance operations and legacy financial services exposure create legacy liabilities

### Business risks
- **Competition**: Rolls-Royce, Pratt & Whitney, and other aero suppliers compete on price, technology, and aftermarket service
- **Long-term service agreements**: 10–25 year commitments create profitability exposure to inflation, tariffs, and operational inefficiencies
- **Execution risk**: Rapid production ramp-up of LEAP engines and new programs requires flawless supply chain coordination and quality
- **Customer concentration**: Boeing and Airbus represent significant revenue concentration; airframe OEM production issues cascade to GE
- **Technology obsolescence**: Next-generation military engines and sustainable aviation fuels require continued R&D investment
- **Cybersecurity**: Critical systems and IP exposure in defense and commercial aviation

## Investment Notes

**Strengths:**
- **Strong demand tailwinds**: Commercial air travel recovery, airline capacity additions, aftermarket expansion, and government defense modernization driving 18-30% revenue growth
- **Recurring revenue model**: 76% of revenue from aftermarket services with 10-25 year contracts; provides visibility and stable margins
- **Margin expansion story**: Operating margin expanded 210 bps to 21.8%; CES segment at 26.6%, DPT at 12.3%, both improving
- **Strong FCF generation**: $7.7B in FCF with 37% conversion of GAAP net income to FCF
- **Healthy balance sheet**: $30.7B net cash; debt well-managed; investing in growth while returning $9B to shareholders
- **Execution momentum**: RPO of $190.6B (4+ years of contracted revenue); production ramping; supply chain improving
- **Government tailwinds**: U.S. DoD modernization, international military sales (new $5B IDIQ contract), defense funding pressure supporting DPT

**Risks to monitor:**
- **Inflation & tariffs**: Noted unfavorable tariff impact on long-term service agreement profitability; labor/material costs elevated
- **Supply chain dependencies**: Material sourcing and component availability still cited as production constraints despite improvements
- **LEAP ramp execution**: Rapid production increase requires flawless supply chain and quality control; any delays impact margins
- **Customer concentration**: Boeing and Airbus account for majority of CES revenue; production issues cascade
- **Competition**: Rolls-Royce, Pratt & Whitney competing aggressively; pricing power tested
- **Long-term service agreement risk**: 10-25 year fixed-margin contracts exposed to inflation, mix, and operational inefficiencies
